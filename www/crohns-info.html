Crohn's Disease

DEFINITION

Crohn’s Disease is an Inflammatory Bowel disease. It can result in inflammation of any part of the gut, from the mouth to the anus. The most common area affected is the end small bowel/small intestine. Often the gut becomes inflamed in patches of varying sizes and severity, with healthy parts of gut in-between these sections.

Crohn’s Disease is a chronic condition. This means that it is ongoing and life-long, although you may have periods of good health (remission), as well as times when symptoms are more active (relapses or flare-ups).


SYMPTOMS

Symptoms vary from person to person. The severity of the symptoms depends on disease activity, the location of the inflammation, the body’s own response to the disease and the influence of medications to fight the disease.

  • Diarrhoea or loose stools
  • Fever (sometimes in the form of night sweats
  • Fatigue, tiredness
  • Anaemia (low blood count)
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Cramps, abdominal pain
  • Fistulae, particularly perianal fistulae (connections or short cuts between the bowel and the skin or the bowel and other organs)
  • Skin tags
  • Weight loss
  • Mouth ulcers
  • Deep ulcers in the bowel or stomach
  • Growth can be affected with children


DEMOGRAPHICS (WHO GETS IT?)

Population based studies from a range of European countries suggest a similar rise in new cases in the adult population.
In Denmark, the mean incident rate for UC in 1995-1998 was 14.4 per 100,000 per year for women and 13.8 for men, increasing to 23.2 per 100,000 per year for women and 23.4 for men in the period of 2009-2011.
The mean incident rate for CD in 1995-1998 was 7.8 per 100,000 per year for women and 5.6 for men, increasing to 10.3 per 100,000 per year for women and 8.9 for men in the period of 2009-2012.
A recent systematic review suggest the highest reported prevalence values for IBD were in Europe (UC, 505 per 100,000 persons; CD, 322 per 100,000 persons) and North America (UC, 249 per 100,000 persons; 310 per 100,000 persons).

Based on these figures, it can be estimated that in 2018 there were approximately 24,259 UC patients and 15,468 CD patients in Ireland (based on population at 2018 census 4,803,748).


CAUSES

The causes for Crohn’s disease are not fully understood at present.
Recent research has pointed to several possible causes for Crohn’s Disease including;

  • An underlying genetic cause
  • Bacteria in the gut causing issues with the immune system resulting in increased inflammation
  • Triggers such as bacteria, viruses, diet, smoking and stress.


TREATMENTS

Treatment of Crohn’s disease can be divided into non-medical, medical and surgical. These treatments depend on the stage and severity of the disease.
Often a combination of all three may be necessary whether controlling active flares or maintaining stable disease.
The aim of these treatments is to heal the inflammation that is causing the above symptoms and help return to a normal quality life, stabilising the disease in the long term. The course of the disease is often unpredictable so treatments can change from time to time where necessary.

  • NON-MEDICAL
    • DIET
    • HEALTH AND FITNESS
    • LIFE STYLE
    • STOPPING SMOKING
  • MEDICAL
    • TABLETS
    • ENEMAS
    • SUPPOSITORIES
    • INJECTIONS
    • INFUSIONS
  • SURGICAL


COMPLICATIONS

There are complications both inside and outside the gut.
Within the gut complications include strictures, perforations and fistulae (see glossary of terms for more information on each).
Complications outside the gut are less common but include;

  • Joints - inflammation of the large and small joints of the arms, legs, pelvis and spine. This can cause pain, swelling and restriction of normal movements in the joints affected. (Arthritis, Sacro-ileitis)
  • Skin - painful, red skin especially on the shins (Erythema Nodosum/Pyoderma)
  • Eyes - painful, red, gritty watery eyes (Iritis, Conjunctivitis)
  • Liver abnormalities due to involvement of the cells (Chronic Active Hepatitis) or bile ducts (Primary Sclerosing Chonangitis)
  • Anaemia (Low blood count)
